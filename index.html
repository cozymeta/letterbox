#001

Planning & Brainstorming

Phase: Brainstorming & sketching in AutoCAD Fusion 360. The goal was to design a letterbox — a hollow sign or 3D text where you place LED lights inside to make it glow.

# brainstorm session — concept sketches
root@letterbox:~$ cat > concepts.txt
- Concept 1: A cat shape — too complex, hard to model cleanly
- Concept 2: A potted plant — theme didn't connect well enough
- Concept 3: Animal Crossing leaf logo + my name — clean, iconic, personal
 root@letterbox:~$ _

I started by brainstorming a few different ideas. The first was a cat shape, but it was too complex to model cleanly. The second was a potted plant, but the theme didn't feel strong enough. I quickly moved on to my final idea: the iconic leaf logo from Animal Crossing, paired with my name as text. It was simple, recognizable, and personal.

A letterbox is basically a hollow sign or 3D text. You put LED lights inside, and the light shines through the letters or shape to make it glow.

  • Final concept: Animal Crossing leaf logo + name text
  • Design type: LED letterbox — hollow sign with internal lighting

#003

Designing

Phase: Taking the sketches into 3D in Fusion 360. Once the sketches were done, it was smooth sailing — I was able to simply extrude them into solid geometry. The real struggle came when I tried to make the words hollow for the LED lights to shine through.

# design log — phase 3
root@letterbox:~$ cat phase3-log.txt
[OK] Sketches extruded successfully
[FAIL] Words not hollow — hubris blocked the tutorial
[FIX] Followed tutorial, got text hollowing working
[WARN] Had to redo 2 sketches — geometry didn't behave

Extruding the sketches was straightforward — just a simple pull into 3D. But hollowing out the words took some trial and error. I tried doing it my own way at first, but after a few failed attempts I relented and followed the tutorial step by step. That did the trick. I also had to remake a couple of sketches that didn't behave as expected during extrusion. In the end I managed to model the base, the caps, and the holes for the LED lights.

#004

Details

Phase: Sending the model to Bamboo Lab's slicer for a preview of how it would 3D print. The preview revealed that some parts weren't printing correctly — overhangs that would sag, thin walls that looked fragile, and geometry that didn't translate well to layers.

▶ FIG 4.1 — Bamboo Lab slicer preview [open window]
  • Bamboo Lab preview — caught printing issues early
  • Fixed geometry — went back and adjusted problem areas
  • Wrong file format — exported to the wrong type, had to re-export

I ran the model through Bamboo Lab's slicer to get a preview of the print. Sure enough, some parts were flagged — overhangs that wouldn't hold up, sections that were too thin. I had to go back to Fusion 360 and make adjustments. On top of that, I initially exported to the wrong file format, which the slicer couldn't read properly. A quick re-export fixed it and the model was finally compatible.

#006

Reflection

Phase: Looking back at the whole process — what worked, what didn't, and what I'd do differently next time.

▶ FIG 6.1 — The final product — design complete [open window]
# retrospective analysis
root@letterbox:~$ ./reflect.sh
[OK] Hubris tamed — followed the tutorial and it worked
[OK] Relearned Fusion 360 — stuck to the formula
[WARN] Still leaving things for the last minute
[WARN] Could have saved time by asking for help earlier
[INFO] Alternative paths are fine, but not at the cost of efficiency
[INFO] Next project: start early, follow instructions, iterate fast
root@letterbox:~$ _

The biggest lesson from this project was learning to stop letting my hubris get the best of me. I kept trying to deviate from the formula, thinking I could find a faster or better way, but it only cost me time. Once I followed the tutorial by the book, everything clicked. I also relearned Fusion 360 along the way — muscle memory came back, but more importantly I learned to trust the process instead of chasing clever shortcuts.

Another thing I need to work on is procrastination. I left a lot of this work for the last minute, which created unnecessary stress and almost resulted in missing pieces. Going forward, I want to start projects earlier and chip away at them consistently rather than cramming at the end. Sometimes the simplest path really is the most efficient one — and there's no shame in taking it.
